Hearing in Slices: The Short-Time Fourier Transform

signal processing
audio
linear algebra
The Fourier transform tells you which frequencies are in a signal. The short-time Fourier transform tells you which frequencies are present when — and why that trade-off is unavoidable.
Author

Andreas Bogossian

Published

April 28, 2026

When you look at the waveform of someone speaking, you see a wiggly line that carries almost no obvious structure. When you look at a spectrogram of the same speech — a picture of frequency content over time — vowels, consonants, and silence all become visually distinct. The spectrogram is what your phone uses when it listens for a wake word, what Shazam computes when it recognises a song, and what audio codecs use to discard frequencies you can’t hear. It is, at its core, a two-dimensional array of Fourier transforms computed on overlapping short windows of the signal. To understand why it works, it helps to start one step earlier.

Signals as vectors

A discrete signal is just a vector \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^N: a sequence of N samples, each one a measurement of amplitude at a point in time. There is nothing special about the standard basis — you can express \mathbf{x} in any basis you like, as long as it spans \mathbb{R}^N. The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) is exactly a change of basis, from time samples to complex exponentials.

The DFT basis vectors are the N complex sinusoids

\mathbf{f}_k = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \begin{bmatrix} 1,\ \omega^k,\ \omega^{2k},\ \ldots,\ \omega^{(N-1)k} \end{bmatrix}^\top, \quad \omega = e^{-2\pi i / N},

stacked as rows of the N \times N DFT matrix \mathbf{F}. Because these basis vectors are orthonormal, \mathbf{F} is unitary: \mathbf{F}\mathbf{F}^* = \mathbf{I}. The transform is therefore a rotation in \mathbb{C}^N:

\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{F}\mathbf{x}.

No information is destroyed. Every coefficient X_k measures how much of the k-th frequency is present in the entire signal, and the inverse \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{F}^*\mathbf{X} recovers the original exactly.

Why complex exponentials specifically? Because they are the eigenfunctions of any linear time-invariant (LTI) system: if e^{i\omega t} enters an LTI system, the output is H(\omega)\,e^{i\omega t} — the same exponential, scaled by a complex constant H(\omega) that depends only on \omega. No frequency mixing ever occurs. This makes complex exponentials the natural language for systems that don’t change over time, which is why the DFT appears in everything from audio filters to differential equation solvers.

The heatmap below makes the DFT basis concrete. Each row of \mathbf{F} is one basis vector; each column is one time sample. Reading across a row, you see how that frequency oscillates over the signal. Reading down a column, you see how all frequencies contribute at a single moment.

omega = np.exp(-2j * np.pi / N)

The punchline: frequency content is already in the signal. The DFT just rotates our perspective so we can see it.

Why global frequency isn’t enough for speech

Pure tones are stationary — their frequency content does not change over time, so the global FFT captures everything useful. Speech is the opposite: it is a succession of rapidly changing sounds, each with its own frequency profile. Vowels carry energy in a few narrow bands called formants; fricatives like /s/ spread energy across high frequencies; stops like /p/ create brief silences followed by bursts.

When you compute the FFT over an entire speech recording, all of these components mix into a single spectrum. You learn which frequencies appear somewhere in the recording, but you lose all information about when they appear. The figure below illustrates this on a chirp — a sinusoid whose frequency sweeps linearly from 200 Hz to 3800 Hz over two seconds.

SR = 8000
duration = 2.0

The chirp’s FFT is essentially flat because the chirp really does visit every frequency — just not at the same time. The global transform cannot distinguish a signal where all frequencies sound at once from one where they occur sequentially. The solution requires tracking frequency content as a function of time, which means the transform must be local.

The STFT: chop, transform, repeat

The fix is conceptually straightforward. Instead of transforming the entire signal at once, multiply it by a short window function w[n], compute the FFT of that windowed chunk, advance the window by a hop size H samples, and repeat. This is the Short-Time Fourier Transform [1]:

X(k, l) = \sum_{n=0}^{W-1} x[kH + n]\, w[n]\, e^{-2\pi i l n / W},

where W is the window length and l = 0, 1, \ldots, W/2 indexes the frequency bin. The result is a 2D array: one axis for time frame k, the other for frequency bin l. The spectrogram is the squared magnitude:

S(k, l) = |X(k, l)|^2.

The window function

The window w[n] does two things. It isolates a short stretch of the signal so the FFT sees only that region. It also tapers smoothly to zero at both ends, which suppresses spectral leakage — the artefact where energy from one frequency “bleeds” into neighbouring bins.

The leakage problem arises because the FFT implicitly assumes the windowed segment repeats periodically. A rectangular window creates a hard edge; any frequency that does not complete an exact integer number of cycles within the window produces a discontinuity, which the FFT interprets as broadband energy. A Hann window’s smooth taper eliminates this discontinuity, concentrating each frequency’s energy into a narrow peak.

N_win = 256
n_arr = np.arange(N_win)

The rectangular window’s first sidelobe sits only 13 dB below the main lobe; a strong tone at one frequency can easily drown out a weaker neighbour. The Hann window pushes the first sidelobe to −32 dB, making it much more reliable in practice.

The resolution trade-off

The window length W governs a fundamental tension. A narrow window localises precisely in time — each frame covers a short interval — but it contains few samples, giving the DFT coarse frequency resolution \Delta f = f_s / W. A wide window gives fine frequency resolution but smears events that are close in time. This is the signal-processing form of the Heisenberg uncertainty principle:

\Delta t \cdot \Delta f \geq \frac{1}{4\pi}.

The tile diagram below shows the geometric consequence. Each tile represents one (k, l) entry of the STFT output: its width is the time resolution, its height the frequency resolution. The total area covered is the same in both cases — only the shape of each tile changes.

The static plot below applies the STFT to the chirp signal we saw earlier. The diagonal stripe is the chirp sweeping from low to high frequency — the temporal structure that was invisible in the global FFT is now plainly visible.

def stft(x, window, hop):
    W = len(window)
    n_frames = (len(x) - W) // hop + 1
    frames = np.stack([x[i*hop : i*hop+W] * window for i in range(n_frames)])
    return np.fft.rfft(frames, axis=1)

W_demo = 256
hop_demo = W_demo // 4
hann_demo = 0.5 * (1 - np.cos(2 * np.pi * np.arange(W_demo) / (W_demo - 1)))

t_frames = np.arange(Zxx.shape[0]) * hop_demo / SR

Inverting the STFT

Because the DFT is unitary, the STFT is in principle invertible. But windowing introduces a complication: the inverse must undo the windowing across all overlapping frames simultaneously. The standard reconstruction is overlap-add (OLA): sum the windowed inverse-DFT outputs for every frame. For this to recover the original signal exactly, the window must satisfy the constant overlap-add property \sum_k w[n - kH] = c for all n. The Hann window satisfies this for any hop size H \leq W/2; with the common choice H = W/4 it holds with constant c = 3/2 [2].

Interactive demo

The demo below makes the resolution trade-off tangible. Drag the window-size slider and watch the spectrogram update: small windows resolve the chirp’s timing but blur its frequency; large windows sharpen the diagonal but smear short events.

N  = SR * 2

  { label: "Signal", value: "Chirp (200 → 3800 Hz)" }
)

viewof windowPow = Inputs.range([5, 10], {
  label: "Window size",
  value: 7,
  step: 1,
  format: v => `2^${v} = ${1 << v} samples  (${((1 << v) / SR * 1000).toFixed(1)} ms)`
})
W   = 1 << windowPow
hop = W >> 2

md`**Time resolution:** ${(hop / SR * 1000).toFixed(1)} ms &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Frequency resolution:** ${(SR / W).toFixed(0)} Hz &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Nyquist:** ${SR / 2} Hz`
signal = {
  const x = new Float64Array(N);
  for (let i = 0; i < N; i++) {
    const ti = i / SR;
    if (signalType === "Chirp (200 → 3800 Hz)") {
      x[i] = Math.sin(2 * Math.PI * (200 * ti + (3600 / (2 * 2.0)) * ti * ti));
    } else if (signalType === "Pure tone (440 Hz)") {
      x[i] = Math.sin(2 * Math.PI * 440 * ti);
    } else if (signalType === "Sum of tones (440 + 1320 Hz)") {
      x[i] = 0.5 * Math.sin(2 * Math.PI * 440 * ti) + 0.5 * Math.sin(2 * Math.PI * 1320 * ti);
    } else {
      const env = 0.5 * (1 + Math.sin(2 * Math.PI * 3.5 * ti));
      x[i] = env       * (0.6 * Math.sin(2 * Math.PI *  300 * ti) +
                          0.3 * Math.sin(2 * Math.PI *  900 * ti) +
                          0.1 * Math.sin(2 * Math.PI * 2400 * ti))
           + (1 - env) * (0.4 * Math.sin(2 * Math.PI * 1200 * ti) +
                          0.4 * Math.sin(2 * Math.PI * 2800 * ti));
    }
  }
  return x;
}
function fft(re, im) {
  const n = re.length;
  let j = 0;
  for (let i = 1; i < n; i++) {
    let bit = n >> 1;
    for (; j & bit; bit >>= 1) j ^= bit;
    j ^= bit;
    if (i < j) {
      let tmp = re[i]; re[i] = re[j]; re[j] = tmp;
          tmp = im[i]; im[i] = im[j]; im[j] = tmp;
    }
  }
  for (let len = 2; len <= n; len <<= 1) {
    const ang = -2 * Math.PI / len;
    const wRe = Math.cos(ang), wIm = Math.sin(ang);
    for (let i = 0; i < n; i += len) {
      let tRe = 1, tIm = 0;
      const half = len >> 1;
      for (let k = 0; k < half; k++) {
        const uRe = re[i+k], uIm = im[i+k];
        const vRe = re[i+k+half]*tRe - im[i+k+half]*tIm;
        const vIm = re[i+k+half]*tIm + im[i+k+half]*tRe;
        re[i+k]      = uRe+vRe; im[i+k]      = uIm+vIm;
        re[i+k+half] = uRe-vRe; im[i+k+half] = uIm-vIm;
        const newTRe = tRe*wRe - tIm*wIm;
        tIm = tRe*wIm + tIm*wRe; tRe = newTRe;
      }
    }
  }
}
stftData = {
  const nFreqs  = (W >> 1) + 1;
  const nFrames = Math.floor((N - W) / hop) + 1;
  const hann    = Float64Array.from({length: W}, (_, i) => 0.5 * (1 - Math.cos(2*Math.PI*i/(W-1))));
  const spec    = new Float64Array(nFrames * nFreqs);
  const re      = new Float64Array(W);
  const im      = new Float64Array(W);

  for (let k = 0; k < nFrames; k++) {
    const start = k * hop;
    for (let n = 0; n < W; n++) { re[n] = signal[start+n]*hann[n]; im[n] = 0; }
    fft(re, im);
    for (let l = 0; l < nFreqs; l++) spec[k*nFreqs+l] = re[l]*re[l] + im[l]*im[l];
  }
  return { spec, nFrames, nFreqs };
}

  ctx.fillRect(0, 0, totalW, totalH);

  // ── spectrogram image ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  const img = ctx.createImageData(innerW, innerH);

  const logSpec = new Float64Array(spec.length);
  for (let i = 0; i < spec.length; i++) logSpec[i] = Math.log1p(spec[i]);
  let maxV = 0;
  for (let i = 0; i < logSpec.length; i++) if (logSpec[i] > maxV) maxV = logSpec[i];

  for (let px = 0; px < innerW; px++) {
    const k = Math.min(nFrames - 1, Math.floor(px / innerW * nFrames));
    for (let py = 0; py < innerH; py++) {
      const l = Math.min(nFreqs - 1, Math.floor((1 - py / innerH) * (nFreqs - 1)));
      const v = maxV > 0 ? logSpec[k * nFreqs + l] / maxV : 0;

      // Inferno-like: black → purple → orange → yellow
      const r = Math.min(255, Math.round(255 * Math.pow(v, 0.45)));
      const g = Math.min(255, Math.round(255 * Math.pow(v, 1.8)));
      const b = Math.min(255, Math.round(255 * (v < 0.5 ? v * 1.2 : Math.max(0, 1.5 - 2 * v))));

      const idx = (py * innerW + px) * 4;
      img.data[idx]   = r; img.data[idx+1] = g;
      img.data[idx+2] = b; img.data[idx+3] = 255;
    }
  }
  ctx.putImageData(img, mL, mT);

  // ── axes ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  ctx.strokeStyle = "#666";
  ctx.lineWidth   = 1;
  ctx.fillStyle   = "#aaa";
  ctx.font        = "11px sans-serif";

  // border around spectrogram
  ctx.strokeRect(mL, mT, innerW, innerH);

  // X-axis ticks (time: 0 … 2 s)
  const timeTicks = [0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0];
  for (const ts of timeTicks) {
    const x = mL + (ts / 2.0) * innerW;
    ctx.beginPath(); ctx.moveTo(x, mT + innerH); ctx.lineTo(x, mT + innerH + 5); ctx.stroke();
    ctx.textAlign = "center";
    ctx.fillText(ts.toFixed(1) + " s", x, mT + innerH + 18);
  }

  const freqTicks = [0, 500, 1000, 1500, 2000, 2500, 3000, 3500, 4000];
  for (const f of freqTicks) {
    const y = mT + innerH - (f / nyquist) * innerH;
    ctx.beginPath(); ctx.moveTo(mL - 5, y); ctx.lineTo(mL, y); ctx.stroke();
    ctx.textAlign = "right";

}

Try sweeping the slider from small to large while watching the chirp. At small windows the diagonal stripe is crisp horizontally — you can pinpoint when the chirp passes a given moment — but the stripe is thick vertically because frequency resolution is coarse. At large windows the stripe sharpens vertically but smears horizontally: nearby time events start to merge. There is no window size that gives you both at once.

Switch to Pure tone: a single horizontal line, as expected, since a stationary sinusoid has constant frequency. Switch to Sum of tones: two horizontal lines whose separation you can resolve with a large window but not a small one. Switch to Speech-like and watch how the amplitude-modulated formants appear and disappear — structure that the global FFT would entirely wash out.

The time-frequency tile

Each entry X(k, l) corresponds to a tile in the time-frequency plane: width H/f_s seconds, height f_s/W Hz. The tiles partition the plane, and their area is fixed at (H/f_s) \cdot (f_s/W) = H/W, a dimensionless quantity that does not depend on the window size — only the aspect ratio changes. This is the geometric expression of the uncertainty bound: you can reshape the tiles, but you cannot shrink them.

Because X(k, l) is a complex number, each tile carries both magnitude and phase. The magnitude squared |X(k,l)|^2 is the energy in that tile, which is what the spectrogram shows. The phase encodes the precise timing of the sinusoidal component within the window and is what enables reconstruction via the inverse STFT.

For multichannel signals — stereo audio, or microphone arrays — there is a natural extension: at each tile (k, l) you collect a vector of complex values \mathbf{x}_{kl} \in \mathbb{C}^C, one per channel. The outer product

\boldsymbol{\Psi}_{kl} = \mathbf{x}_{kl}\mathbf{x}_{kl}^*

is a C \times C covariance matrix that encodes the cross-channel phase and amplitude relationships at that moment in time and frequency. This single matrix contains everything about the spatial relationship between signals at frequency l during frame k: it tells you where the sound came from, whether multiple sources are active, and how correlated they are. Processing these per-tile covariance matrices is the basis of modern spatial audio coding; the framework for doing so optimally — in the sense of minimum mean-square error — is developed in [3].

The spectrogram, then, is not just a visualisation aid. It is the natural representation for non-stationary signals: a compact, invertible decomposition that exposes local frequency content at every moment in time, at a resolution bounded by physics.
