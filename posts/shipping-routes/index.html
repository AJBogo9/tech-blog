Global Shipping Routes

visualization
interactive
three.js
An interactive 3D globe visualising the world’s major container shipping routes, built with Three.js.
Author

Andreas Bogossian

Published

April 19, 2026

About 90% of world trade moves by sea. The container shipping network that makes this possible is one of the most complex logistics systems ever built — a mesh of fixed routes, seasonal detours, and just-in-time schedules that connects nearly every port on Earth. At its core are a handful of very high-volume corridors: Asia–Europe through the Suez Canal, transpacific lanes to the US West Coast, and the transatlantic run between Europe and the Americas.

The demo below renders 12 of those routes on an interactive globe. Each arc follows a great-circle path, which is the shortest distance between two points on a sphere — the same geometry that real vessels target before currents and canal access reshape their actual track. The moving dots are ships in transit; each one loops its route continuously so you can see how traffic concentrates on a few key corridors.

Controls: drag to rotate · scroll to zoom · hover a port marker for its name.

The 12 routes shown account for the bulk of containerised freight by volume. The Asia–Europe corridor (Shanghai / Singapore / Colombo → Rotterdam / Hamburg) is the world’s single busiest lane. The transpacific corridors (Shanghai, Busan, Yokohama → Los Angeles) are not far behind. What looks like a web of independent links is actually a hub-and-spoke system: Rotterdam, Singapore, and Shanghai act as mega-hubs that aggregate and redistribute cargo from dozens of feeder routes not shown here.
